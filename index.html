01 — What the Company Does
\n

The Business

\n\n

Time Technoplast is an Indian multinational that makes containers — but the simplicity of that description hides a genuinely complicated business. Founded in 1989 and incorporated in 1992, the company began with a sharp and contrarian bet: that Indian industry would eventually abandon the heavy, corrosive, price-volatile steel drum in favour of plastic. Thirty-five years later, that bet has paid off handsomely. The company is now the world's largest manufacturer of large-size plastic drums, holds a 50-60% share of the domestic industrial packaging market, and runs manufacturing facilities across 11 countries from its headquarters in Andheri, Mumbai.

\n\n

But calling this a packaging company does not quite capture what Time Technoplast has become. The original drum and jerry can business still generates the bulk of revenue, but over the past decade the company has been climbing a technology ladder — from blow-moulded drums, up through 1,000-litre intermediate bulk containers, and now into composite cylinders that store CNG, LPG, hydrogen, and oxygen at high pressure. These composite products are technically complex, capital-intensive to build, and command margins roughly 500-600 basis points higher than the core packaging business.

\n\n

Time Technoplast was promoted by a group of qualified professionals, not legacy industrialists — people who understood polymer processing as a technical discipline. Anil Jain, the co-founder who served as Managing Director until his death in February 2022, built the company on the premise of replacing metal with plastic wherever physics allowed. His successor Bharat Kumar Vageria continues that philosophy with particular focus on composite materials, where the substrate shifts from simple plastic to carbon fibre-wrapped polymer liners operating at hundreds of bar of pressure.

\n\n

The concrete mechanics of the business: a specialty chemical company in Vadodara needs to ship 200 litres of a solvent to Bangkok. It needs a container that won't react with the chemical, won't corrode in transit, meets UN hazardous goods standards, and can be reused multiple times. Time Technoplast sells it an HDPE drum from one of its 16 Indian plants or 10 overseas facilities — possibly from a plant located near the chemical manufacturer's own site to reduce logistics costs. That same Bangkok customer likely buys from Time Technoplast's Thailand facility. This geographic proximity model is central to why Time Technoplast wins against European competitors: Mauser and Schutz are global giants but Time Technoplast has planted itself inside the demand clusters of Asia and MENA, making delivered cost economics very difficult to beat.

\n\n

On the composite cylinder side, the stakes are higher. A CNG fuel station needs large cascade storage vessels — cylinders that hold natural gas at 250 bar pressure. Time Technoplast's Type-IV composite cascade stores twice the gas at the same external size, weighs 60-70% less, and pays back the higher upfront cost to the fuel station operator in under 12 months through faster fuelling times and higher throughput. This is a step-change in the economics of CNG infrastructure, not a marginal improvement.

02 — Business Segments
\n

The Segments

\n\n

Time Technoplast officially reports two financial segments: Polymer Products (~64% of revenue) and Composite Products (~36%). But management thinks about the business through two lenses internally — Established Products (73%) and Value-Added Products or VAP (27%). The VAP classification cuts across both reporting segments and is the one that matters most for understanding the company's trajectory.

\n\n

2a. Industrial Packaging — The Foundation

\n

Industrial packaging covers polymer drums, jerry cans, pails, and the broader drum-and-container universe. Capacities range from 5 to 250 litres. Products are manufactured through blow moulding, injection moulding, and extrusion — producing seamless, weld-free containers with no weak points across 16 domestic and 10 international manufacturing locations.

\n\n

The 50-60% domestic market share in large-size plastic drums is a function of 35 years of accumulated capacity, customer approvals, and geographic density. Industrial packaging customers are conservative — they qualify a supplier, validate the drum against their chemical specification, and stay. The approval process takes 12-24 months. Once in, a supplier is hard to dislodge. Time Technoplast has built 900+ customer relationships on this basis, with marquee accounts including:

This segment operates at EBITDA margins of approximately 13%. Volume growth has been steady in the 8-12% range through the business cycle. The growing shift from drums to IBCs (1,000-litre intermediate bulk containers) is an additional tailwind within this segment — IBC growth runs at 18-20% annually and carries a higher margin than drums.

\n\n

2b. Composite Cylinders — The Growth Engine

\n

Time Technoplast manufactures Type-IV composite cylinders — a thin polymer liner (HDPE or nylon) wound with layers of carbon fibre or fiberglass, then cured under heat and pressure. Lighter, stronger per unit weight, and more corrosion-resistant than any steel alternative. It was the first company in India to commercialise Type-IV composite cylinders for both LPG and CNG.

\n\n

The composite cylinder business covers four distinct product lines:

\n
    \n
  • LPG cylinders — 2 kg to 20 kg, ~85% utilisation, exported to 48 countries. Revenue ~Rs. 240-250 Cr at current capacity. India has 50 crore LPG cylinders in circulation, all currently steel.
    • \n
  • CNG cascades — Highest-value product. 60 cylinders per cascade, ~Rs. 1 crore per composite cascade vs Rs. 20 lakh for steel with half the capacity. Payback period under 12 months for fuel station operators. New Vapi plant adds 600 cascades/yr capacity.
    • \n
  • Oxygen and SCBA cylinders — Hospitals, emergency services. Similar margin profile to LPG/CNG.
    • \n
  • Hydrogen cylinders — Proof-of-concept stage. First trial order (Rs. 2.30 Cr for Indian Armed Forces, 200 kg at 250 bar) secured Feb 2026. Commercial production from Q1 FY27.
    • \n
\n\n

The composite segment generates EBITDA margins of approximately 18-19%, compared to 13% for established products. Each 1% shift in composite revenue contribution lifts blended EBITDA margin by approximately 20-30 basis points.

\n\n

2c. Infrastructure Products — The Steady Earner

\n

HDPE pipes branded Max'M for water supply, drainage, sewage, and power/communication cable ducts. Plants in Silvassa, Hyderabad, and Kolkata. Generated approximately Rs. 280 Cr in FY25. Management has guided 20%+ CAGR over three years, driven by Jal Jeevan Mission. Active order book of over Rs. 200 Cr. New multilayer pipes with silicone in-lining for smart city cable ducts — early-stage, meaningful medium-term opportunity.

\n\n

2d. Technical, Automotive, and Lifestyle

\n

Auto components: deaeration tanks (Tech DAT), spray suppression systems for commercial vehicles (3S Rainflaps), plastic fuel tanks (TechTank), and air ducts — sold to commercial vehicle OEMs. The 3S rainflap business has a regulatory tailwind as vehicle safety mandates tighten. Lifestyle products (Duro mats and turf, Regal furniture, Dumpo bins) generate Rs. 200+ Cr through 50+ distributors at 15%+ EBITDA margins — a B2C business in an otherwise B2B company.

\n\n

2e. Energy Storage — Non-Core, Earmarked for Exit

\n

PowerBuild Batteries (formerly NED Energy, 97%-owned subsidiary) makes lead-acid batteries for railway, telecom, solar, and industrial use. Revenue ~Rs. 125 Cr. Identified as non-core; management has guided potential divestiture within 2-3 years once the business achieves better scale. Two new products in development: transparent container batteries for railways and low-cost batteries for e-rickshaws.

03 — Products and Business Detail
\n

Products and Manufacturing

\n\n

The Full Product Catalogue

\n

On the packaging side: drums and barrels from 5 to 250 litres in HDPE and other polymers, open-top and closed-head configurations, the XL-Ring drum for UN-rated hazardous goods, conipails, narrow-mouth polycans for liquids, and coin-pack pails for retail lubricants. There is a packaging development team that creates custom containers for specific customer applications.

\n\n

IBCs come in standard 1,000-litre configurations in the GNX brand, manufactured at Silvassa, Dahej, and the US facility in Chicago. MOX films (Multi-Oriented eXtruded) are cross-laminated industrial films under the Techpaulin brand — stronger than standard plastic films, used for industrial tarps, transport covers, bags, and raincoats. Time Technoplast is the second-largest MOX film maker in India.

\n\n

Composite cylinders span LPG (2 kg to 20 kg), CNG (cascades and onboard vehicle), oxygen, SCBA, hydrogen (200 bar and 250 bar), and composite fire extinguisher (T4-FireGuard). Developing 250-litre and 350-litre onboard CNG cylinders for vehicles.

\n\n

Manufacturing Process — Blow Moulding (Drums)

\n

HDPE granules are fed into an extruder that melts and homogenises the resin. The melt is pushed into a parison (a tube of molten plastic) that drops into an open mould. Compressed air inflates the parison against the mould walls, cooling into the drum shape. The process is continuous, automated, and produces seamless containers without welds. Cycle times are measured in seconds. Time Technoplast runs multi-cavity moulds on continuous production lines — this is the primary driver of its cost advantage over smaller players.

\n\n

Manufacturing Process — Composite Cylinders (Type-IV)

\n

A polymer liner (HDPE or polyamide nylon) is blow-moulded to the cylinder's inner shape, then mounted on a rotating mandrel in a filament winding machine. Carbon fibre tow is fed through an epoxy resin bath and wound onto the liner at precisely calculated angles — helical winds for axial strength, hoop winds for burst pressure resistance. The winding pattern is an engineering calculation: for a 250-bar hydrogen cascade, it took years to validate. After winding, the cylinder enters an oven cure cycle. Boss fittings are moulded into the liner before winding. Every finished cylinder is tested hydrostatically to 150% of working pressure and must be traceable to its raw material batch.

04 — Customers
\n

Customers

\n\n

Time Technoplast is a B2B business for approximately 95% of revenue. The remaining 5% is B2C through lifestyle products distribution.

\n\n

Who Buys and Why

\n

The industrial packaging customer base spans specialty chemicals (31% of revenue), FMCG (29%), construction chemicals (13%), paints and inks (12%), pharmaceuticals (6%), lube oil and additives (4%), and others. Buyers are procurement managers and supply chain directors at large manufacturing companies. The buying decision involves three stages: regulatory qualification (does the drum meet UN hazardous goods standards?), quality validation (field trials, leakage tests, chemical compatibility), and commercial negotiation. Once a supplier passes the first two stages, the relationship tends to be sticky.

\n\n

For large MNC customers like Dow or BASF, the qualification is global — a drum approved for use with a specific chemical in India can be specified across all countries where that same chemical is packed. This is why Time Technoplast's relationship with Dow has extended to 7 countries: the qualification was done once, and the multi-country footprint allows Dow to use a single specification globally.

\n\n

Switching Costs

\n

Switching a packaging supplier is not trivial. At minimum, the new supplier's drums must be re-qualified against the chemical being packed — for hazardous goods, this requires UN-certified testing and often takes 6-12 months. For pharmaceutical customers, the switch requires regulatory filings in some jurisdictions. For FMCG and food customers, a new drum shape or dimension means adjusting automated filling equipment. These barriers introduce strong inertia. In practice, customers maintain 2-3 approved suppliers and distribute volumes between them rather than single-sourcing.

\n\n

Customer Concentration

\n

No single customer exceeds 5% of consolidated revenue, which management has stated explicitly. The 900+ institutional customer base is diversified across sector and geography. The largest accounts are all global chemical or FMCG companies — Dow, BASF, Shell, Solvay, Ecolab — but cumulatively represent at most 20-25% of revenue given the wallet share percentages disclosed.

\n\n

Composite Cylinder Customers

\n

CNG fuel station operators, City Gas Distribution (CGD) companies, OMCs running LPG distribution, industrial gas users, and increasingly defence and strategic users. The buying dynamic here is project-based for cascades (a fuel station might order 20-50 cascades at a time) and recurring for LPG cylinders (IOCL orders in tranches of hundreds of thousands).

Composite Cylinders — India Market

\n

In composite cylinders, the competitive situation is fundamentally different from packaging. Globally, serious Type-IV composite cylinder manufacturers include Hexagon (Norway), Luxfer (UK), and Worthington Industries (USA). In India, the market is currently a duopoly: Time Technoplast and Supreme Industries. Both hold PESO approvals; Time Technoplast has a larger first-mover advantage in CNG cascades and has historically dominated order flow from CGD companies.

\n\n

Entry barriers for composite cylinders are substantially higher than for packaging: PESO certification (1-2 years per product variant), specialised filament winding equipment (imported, 12-18 month delivery lead times), carbon fibre supply chain (few global suppliers, import-dependent), a composite-trained workforce (rare in India), and a safety track record that customers require before trusting pressure-critical products.

06 — Industry
\n

Industry

\n\n

Demand Drivers

\n

The packaging business is a derivative of industrial production volumes — the more chemical, FMCG, paint, and pharmaceutical output India and Asia produce, the more packaging is needed. India's specialty chemicals industry is growing at 11-13% annually, driven by China+1 supply chain diversification. FMCG volumes in India and Southeast Asia are tied to consumption growth and urbanisation — structural, decade-long tailwinds.

\n\n

The shift from steel to polymer packaging is an additional structural tailwind. Polymer drums account for approximately 58% of drum usage in India but only 19% globally. As Asia's chemical industry matures and environmental regulations around steel drum disposal tighten, polymer adoption continues to rise.

\n\n

For composite cylinders, demand is driven by India's CNG infrastructure expansion under PNGRB regulations, LPG distribution modernisation, industrial gas applications, and the emerging hydrogen economy. The government's National Green Hydrogen Mission (5 million metric tonnes by 2030) creates a future demand pool for storage infrastructure that will require composite cylinders.

\n\n

Industry Size

\n

The global industrial packaging market is estimated to reach $123.2 billion by 2032 at a 5.9% CAGR. The rigid IBC segment alone is $4.2 billion in 2025, growing at 5.8%. India's polymer drum market is approximately 16 million units domestically.

\n\n

Regulatory Environment

\n

The Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation (PESO) governs cylinders in India. PESO approval is required for any new cylinder product, any new manufacturing facility, and any modification to existing certified designs. Time Technoplast has accumulated approvals for LPG cylinders in multiple sizes, CNG cascades, Type-III and Type-IV configurations, oxygen/SCBA cylinders, and now Type-3 cylinders (January 2026). Each additional approval protects the position because every competitor must go through the same process.

\n\n

Cyclicality

\n

The packaging business is largely non-cyclical — chemicals, FMCG, pharmaceuticals, and food are essential industries. The pipe business has cyclicality tied to government capex and EPC contractor procurement (evident in FY23 when polymer price spikes caused EPC delays). The composite cylinder business has a lumpy, order-book-driven character that creates quarterly volatility even when the underlying demand trend is positive.

08 — Key Risks
\n

Key Risks

\n\n
\n

Risk 1 — LPG Composite Cylinder Ramp: OMC Dependency

\n

The single biggest swing factor in the composite business is whether and how fast Indian OMCs (IOCL, BPCL, HPCL) shift LPG cylinder procurement from steel to composite. These three state-owned companies effectively control the 50-crore-cylinder LPG distribution system in India. Time Technoplast and Supreme together have capacity for only 7-8% of the annual replacement requirement. If OMCs were to mandate composite, demand would overwhelm current capacity by 10x. The risk is they don't move — the OMC procurement apparatus is slow and subject to political considerations (steel drum manufacturers and state-owned steel companies have their own lobby). Assessment: high probability, material drag — not catastrophic but capable of keeping the LPG business at a fraction of potential for years longer than management guides.

\n
\n\n
\n

Risk 2 — Raw Material Price Volatility and Pass-Through Lag

\n

Polymer pricing (HDPE, PP, LLDPE) is volatile and tied to crude oil cycles. Time Technoplast's revenue contains a pass-through mechanism but with a 1-3 month lag. During periods of rapid polymer price increases, margins compress before the pass-through catches up. This was visible in FY23 (HDPE price spikes) and in FY26 (polymer prices falling suppressed revenue growth even as volumes were healthy). The mechanism creates symmetric risk in both directions. Assessment: ongoing, recurring, manageable — creates quarterly noise but not structural damage.

\n
\n\n
\n

Risk 3 — Composite Cylinder Capex Cycle Risk

\n

Filament winding equipment is imported, takes 12-18 months to procure, and costs substantially more than packaging equipment. If the company commissions capacity in anticipation of demand that is slower to materialise — CGD rollouts delayed, hydrogen spending pushed out — the fixed cost of running underutilised composite capacity will drag on returns. The CNG cascade business has historically had lumpy orders tied to CGD operators' own capital allocation cycles. A sustained slowdown in CGD expansion — which has been repeatedly affected by gas pricing disputes — would reduce composite volume meaningfully without affecting fixed costs proportionally.

\n
\n\n
\n

Risk 4 — Key Man Risk (MD Concentration)

\n

Bharat Kumar Vageria is simultaneously MD and CFO — a concentration of executive authority notable for a company of this size. He runs all four concalls, answers virtually every analyst question, and has been the primary external face since Anil Jain's death in February 2022. The company has not publicly articulated a succession plan or clear second-tier leadership.

\n
\n\n
\n

Risk 5 — Geopolitical Exposure in MENA

\n

Approximately 10-12% of consolidated revenue comes from Middle East operations (UAE, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, Egypt). In March 2026, the company filed a BSE notice confirming \"normal operations despite Middle East conflicts\" — suggesting management was proactively reassuring investors. This exposure creates ongoing risk of operational disruption, import restrictions, or currency dislocations (Egypt's pound has been under severe pressure).

\n
\n\n
\n

Risk 6 — Promoter Stake Dilution

\n

Promoter holding declined 4.11% in the most recent quarter to 47.5%. While the QIP diluted all shareholders proportionally, any ongoing promoter selling would warrant attention. Promoters had previously stated all capital is in Time Technoplast — a shift in that stance would be a signal.

\n
\n\n
\n

Risk 7 — Hydrogen and New Energy: Long Gestation

\n

Hydrogen cylinders, drone applications, and broader clean energy ambitions require 5-10 years to generate meaningful revenue. The first commercial order (Rs. 2.30 Cr for Indian Armed Forces) is genuinely small. If green hydrogen development in India is slower than mission targets — a plausible outcome given cost structures — capital deployed in hydrogen cylinder capacity will earn below-normal returns for an extended period.

\n
\n\n\n\n
09 — Walk the Talk
\n

Management Credibility

\n\n

Time Technoplast's concall record across four quarters reveals a management that is broadly consistent in medium-term framing, reasonably accurate on operational delivery, but regularly optimistic on the pace of composite business ramp — with a history of pushing timelines on specific projects.

\n\n

Q4 FY25 Concall (May 28, 2025) — Setting the Stage

\n

Vageria entered this call having guided the prior year for a ROCE of 18% in FY25. The result was delivered: ROCE came in at 18.1%, the fourth consecutive year of hitting the ROCE target he had set publicly three years earlier. This is an important data point. He also guided composite growth of 28-30% for FY26 and described debt-free status as achievable \"within 18 months\" based on operating cash flow alone.

\n\n
\n \"In the next 20 days I can raise and be debt free. But the management decision is a prudent decision, because when you are getting the cost of the fund at 9%, so when ROCE we have to give at 20%, so by raising equity we cannot...\"\n Q4 FY25 concall, May 28, 2025 — on why the company hadn't rushed into QIP\n
\n\n

Yet the QIP was raised in November 2025, just six months later — and once raised, the debt repayment was faster than the organic timeline suggested. On the QIP timing, the pivot was pragmatic and strategically sound, even if the stated rationale in prior calls implied a longer wait.

\n\n

Q1 FY26 Concall (August 12, 2025) — Composite Expansion Timeline

\n

Vageria said the new CNG/hydrogen composite plant near Vapi would be commissioned in \"the second half of this year\" (H2 FY26). He attributed delays to construction holdups from the rainy season. In Q4 FY26, the company announced this plant was indeed commissioned — with trial production begun. So the half-year delay in the commissioning announcement was within an acceptable range. He also stated E-Rickshaw battery approvals were expected \"in next 30-45 days.\" There is no publicly available confirmation that this was received on schedule — it either slipped or management moved on without updating investors prominently. A minor credibility gap on a secondary business.

\n\n

Q2 FY26 Concall (November 17, 2025) — Post-QIP

\n

The QIP was completed at the start of November. The Rs. 800 Cr raise and swift deployment of Rs. 321 Cr to debt repayment is consistent with prior guidance. The interest cost line has been declining steadily — from Rs. 22.47 Cr in Q3 FY25 to Rs. 18.83 Cr in Q3 FY26 — demonstrating the debt reduction is flowing through to profitability. Management's execution on the QIP and subsequent debt reduction was clean and fast.

\n\n

Q3 FY26 Concall (February 13, 2026) — Composite Growth Validated

\n

Q3 FY26 reported 15% revenue growth in value-added products with 18.8% EBITDA margin. VAP's share of total revenue rose to 30% from 27% a year earlier — directionally consistent with the 35%-in-3-years trajectory guided since Q4 FY25. However, the large government LPG order referenced in Q4 FY25 as \"delayed\" had still not been publicly confirmed as received by Q3 FY26. Management's discussion of OMC progress has been consistently vague, and the timeline on when OMC volumes might jump from small to meaningful remains undefined. This is the one area where \"we will deliver\" language has been repeated most consistently without a definitive outcome.

\n\n

Overall Assessment

\n

Vageria runs a tight ship on ROCE management and operational delivery. He set a four-year ROCE roadmap and hit every year. He raised the QIP when capital market conditions allowed. He has been honest about OMC uncertainty and has not promised what he cannot control. The main gap in the credibility record is the E-Rickshaw battery approval timeline and the prolonged ambiguity around OMC LPG volumes. These are secondary businesses, but they illustrate a pattern of optimistic timelines on new or government-dependent revenue streams. The core business guidance — volume growth, EBITDA margins, ROCE targets — has been accurate and consistently delivered.

Bull Case
\n

Everything that Time Technoplast has been quietly building for the past decade comes together. The Vapi composite plant runs to full capacity within 18 months of commissioning, generating the Rs. 700 Cr CNG and hydrogen revenue Vageria has described. One or more OMCs — perhaps BPCL, which has been further along in discussions — issues a large-format order for composite LPG cylinders, creating a step-change in the composite revenue line. The company becomes net debt-free by Q2 FY27 as guided, eliminating Rs. 70-80 Cr of annual interest expense and directly boosting profits.

\n

VAP's share of revenue crosses 33-34% by FY27 and approaches 40% by FY28. The margin expansion from this mix shift is real — every percentage point of VAP is 500+ basis points higher EBITDA margin, and the operating leverage in the composite business amplifies volume growth into profit growth. The Silvassa Phase-II IBC expansion adds capacity just as Indian chemical sector growth hits another gear. Overseas packaging continues compounding at 15-17% as Southeast Asia's chemical industry deepens.

\n

The hydrogen story — today a Rs. 2.30 Cr trial order — begins to yield meaningful revenue from FY28 as the National Green Hydrogen Mission catalyses infrastructure buildout. The drone cylinder MOU validates and scales. Time Technoplast becomes the go-to composite cylinder manufacturer for defence, energy, and industrial applications in India, analogous to what Hexagon Composites has become in Norway. The company re-rates as a high-growth industrials company with clear ESG positioning in clean energy infrastructure.

\n
\n\n
\n
Base Case
\n

The company delivers approximately what management has guided but at a measured pace. The composite business grows 25-30% annually, reaching 32-33% of revenue by FY27, but OMC orders remain episodic rather than transformational. The IBC business grows 18-20% and becomes increasingly meaningful. Established products grow at 10-12% with steady margin improvement from automation and solar savings.

\n

The company becomes net debt-free by FY27 or early FY28, slightly behind the Q1 FY27 target given working capital needs. Interest cost savings add to profit margins. ROCE reaches 20-21% in FY26 as guided and continues expanding. VAP share reaches 30-32% by FY27. The battery business is quietly divested for a modest premium, cleaning up the balance sheet further.

\n

In this scenario, Time Technoplast is a mid-teen revenue growth company with margin expansion from mix and efficiency, a cleaner balance sheet, and incrementally better return metrics. The composite transformation is real but gradual. The hydrogen optionality remains optionality.

\n
\n\n
\n
Bear Case
\n

The OMC LPG order flow that management has been anticipating fails to materialise at scale. A new government procurement policy delays the composite LPG inflection for another 3-5 years. The Vapi composite plant ramps slowly, running at 40-50% utilisation in FY27 and absorbing fixed costs without proportional revenue. Composite revenue growth guided at 28-30% comes in at 15-18%.

\n

Simultaneously, global polymer prices spike again as crude oil rises and supply chain disruptions recur. The pass-through mechanism works with a lag, compressing margins for 2-3 quarters. In MENA, a regional geopolitical escalation disrupts the Bahrain or UAE operations for a quarter. The HDPE pipe business sees further EPC procurement delays as Jal Jeevan Mission timelines slip.

\n

The battery business turns into a distraction rather than being quietly monetised — the planned divestiture takes longer than expected, the new products don't reach commercial scale, and management attention is split. The promised debt-free status slips to FY28. Revenue growth slows to 8-10%, EBITDA margins plateau, and the re-rating story fails to materialise. The packaging core remains solid, but investors who paid a premium for the composite growth story are left with a business that looks more like an industrial packaging company than an energy transition play.
